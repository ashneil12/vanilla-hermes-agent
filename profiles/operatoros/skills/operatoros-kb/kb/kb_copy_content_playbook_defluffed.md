## 1) The 80/20 rules (anti-fluff)

### Dry’s 3-question quality gate
1) Can I visualize it?  
2) Can I falsify it?  
3) Can nobody else say this?

## The Three Rules of Effective Copywriting

You have these three rules:
1.  **Can I visualize it?**
2.  **Can I falsify it?**
3.  **Can nobody else say this?**

So you get three nos, you've probably written a lot of rubbish. You get three yeses, you're onto something.

### Precision > persuasion
## Part 6: Facts, Precision, and Communication

### Steinbeck rule
## Coda: The Steinbeck Method (Uncommon Effect with Common Language)

**Interviewer:** In the world of food, so much of what I read is overdone and overwritten. I think of cookbooks, I think of elaborate descriptions on bougie restaurant menus. And I don't know about you, but all that has deceived me into thinking that writing about food has to be complex in order for it to be successful. But I've come to realize that's just not true. You don't need fancy language. Common language works just fine.

For an example, I want to look at John Steinbeck and his masterpiece *East of Eden*. As I read this paragraph, I want you to notice the simplicity of the language here:

> **The apple pie was golden and fragrant, its crust delicately brown and sugar-crusted, with the faintest hint of cinnamon wafting up. The apples inside were tender but not mushy, each bite offering a balance of sweet and tart that made my mouth water. The warmth of the pie, coupled with a scoop of melting vanilla ice cream, made each mouthful a little piece of heaven, evoking memories of long and lazy autumn afternoons.**

First of all, that is beautiful writing. Second of all, I'm hungry. This paragraph is alive, isn't it? And the words are so simple. There's nothing pretentious about it.

Let's break it down. Steinbeck's description comes alive because it's so layered. He's appealing to multiple senses: sight, smell, touch, taste.
* You can see the **golden** and **delicately brown** crust.
* You can practically touch the **warm pie** and the **melting vanilla ice cream**.
* You can taste the faint scent of **cinnamon** and the **tender but not mushy** apples.

It's the collection of these varied descriptions that's giving this paragraph life.

Then there's the pacing. Steinbeck wants you to linger on the details. Look at the structure: three sentences, and the whole paragraph is a single fluid, continuous thought. Every sentence is roughly the same length, which gives every sentence equal weighting. There are a total of seven commas, serving as gentle pauses because Steinbeck is asking you as the reader to just slow down and feel the deliberate and unhurried pace.

Finally, there's the sequencing, which pulls you into the intimacy of the moment. It's like a slow zoom-in shot in a film, where he's starting from far away and just moving closer and closer. He's going from eye to mouth to mind.

If there's one takeaway I want you to remember, it's this: there's no need to show off with super-duper fancy $100 words. You can have an uncommon effect on your reader by using common language to describe a common experience, just like Steinbeck.

## 2) Process (so drafts happen)

### Drafting workflow (Dry)
## Part 4: The Copywriting Process Framework

**Interviewer:** So you sit down. Okay, I'm Harry, I'm going to sit down and write some copy. Tell me how your process then unfolds from there.

**Harry Dry:** I start with what am I trying to do here. There's a quote by John Hegarty: "The current attitude of the consumer is the starting point, and the desired attitude is the finish line. You can't start a race in the middle." So when I sit down to write, I'm thinking, what's the current attitude (Point A)? What's the desired attitude (Point B)? It's my job to get the customer from A to B.

How do I string the wire? There are three pieces here.

### Long-form template (Hormozi)
1) Story  
2) Principle  
3) Examples  
4) “What to do next”

### Chapter Structure and Workflow

Each of the chapters I tend to have the same structure because I write the way I would like to read. I write non-fiction, so I like to have some sort of narrative or story that kind of puts context to what I'm talking about. This just gives color to it.

My chapter structure:
1.  **Narrative/Story:** Provides context for the principle.
2.  **Short Description:** A very short description of the thing that I'm going to be talking about.
3.  **Plentiful Examples:** To illustrate the concept.
4.  **Alex Notes:** A summary or application section at the end.

I pretty much stuck with that setup for all of the books that I've written, and I think that setup has just gotten cleaner and clearer between the books because they fundamentally are like my notes brought to life in a book format.

The hardest part for me is usually picking what story I want to tell in each chapter that best embodies whatever the principle is, and what visual framework I can tie to that that kind of melts everything together. I usually do words first, and then I'll put these highlighted caps marks where I'll say, "A picture that looks like this," and then I'll move on. I basically do words first, then I'll go back through, I'll keep cleaning words, and then I'll put rough doodles in. Only at the very end will I come in and put the final doodles because sometimes my order changes, and I'll put numbers in a doodle that if I move the paragraph around, I have to redo it.

But that's been my overall process for writing. I just write as much as I can until I can't write anymore, where I feel like my words-per-unit-of-time starts to drop pretty precipitously.

## 3) Hooks + pain-first conversion

### Hooks (Hormozi)
### Hooks and Editing

We will look at other industries that have higher performing videos and look at packaging that seems to have performed well and say, "Is there a business version of this that we could do?" The biggest leverage on performance for a video is going to be hook, packaging, title, thumbnail, and first 30-60 seconds. We have to solve for congruence: is the thing they clicked on the thing that they're going to get?

When editing, I look at it like coats of paint. I let it breathe, then come back to it and do another coat. The vast majority of my editing is me crunching things down. How can I use fewer words? How can I use simple words? I keep doing that until I feel like anything else that I would remove would materially detract from the substance. That process right there is usually like 10 drafts. For *Leads*, it was 19 drafts, and I basically started from scratch at about halfway through those.

### Pain is the pitch (Hormozi)
### The Pain is the Pitch

**Alex Hormozi:** You fundamentally have two methods of persuasion: more good stuff (promise) or less bad stuff (pain). My goal is to highlight both of those. From a selling perspective, you make an offer at the point of greatest deprivation, not at the point of greatest satisfaction.

* **Steakhouse Analogy:** If you're incredibly hungry, you come to my steakhouse, I say "Do you want a steak?" You eat it, love it. I say, "Awesome, do you want another steak?" You'd be like, "No, I'm good." I'm trying to sell at the point of greatest satisfaction. But if you walk in starving and I say, "Want two steaks?" You might say yes. We sell at the point of greatest pain.

* **Application:** People often overemphasize promise. Pain is what motivates a lot of people to take action. If you can accurately describe a prospect's pain in their own language, in their own experiences, you can persuade them to buy. They're like, "You can't know that pain this well and not be able to deliver."

When I wrote a sales page for [my wife] Leila's story, I thought about how she would wear a coverall when she would go to the beach, and she would get chafing between her thighs when she was walking all day because she was overweight; not wanting to be in pictures. Pain happens in moments. I want to capture the moment.

## 4) Ads + landing pages diagnostics

### Two-Mississippi test (Dry)
## Part 3: Ad Analysis and The "Two Mississippi" Test

**Interviewer:** Tell me about this ad: "They don't write songs about Volvos."

**Harry Dry:** It's an ad by Corvette. Six words. A lot of white space. Firstly, again, we've got an enemy. They're selling the Corvette by having a little cheap shot at Volvo. But secondly, what can Corvette say about the Corvette that no other car can? They can say that Post Malone sings about Corvettes. All these American country singers love a song about a Corvette.

What's good about this, and I think what's good about all copywriting, is you get it instantly. There's an intuitive test with copywriting. Sometimes you know more in 2 seconds than you know in 2 hours. Let me give you this test. I can't remember who came up with it, but it's called **One Mississippi, Two Mississippi**. I got this ad, I show it to you. One Mississippi, two Mississippi. You get it. If it takes longer than two seconds, you know...

**Interviewer:** You printed this one out [Athletic Greens ad: "You're going to need a smaller cabinet"], I did not get it. I did one Mississippi, two Mississippi, 8,000 Mississippi, and I did not get it. What is going on in this ad?

**Harry Dry:** "You're going to need a smaller cabinet." Athletic Greens. What does Athletic Greens replace? Positioning 101: what do you replace? That to me says this does everything in one go. What's the pain point? Morning routine: I want vitamin A, vitamin D, vitamin C, I want some zinc. You don't need a cabinet, just all in one.

**Interviewer:** Do you think that sometimes there's a risk that you're going to do something that really relates to your in-crowd, but then the out-crowd won't get it? Because I don't get this, but I don't take vitamins like that.

**Harry Dry:** Yeah, I think if everyone gets it, you're possibly doing something wrong. I think that's not a problem at all. What's funny is you don't get it, but you talked about it. It got your attention. What's worse is something which is just like... a bad way of doing that ad might be "One drink to replace them all." You know, it's been said. You want to write something that no one said before.

## 5) Editing (defluff checklist)

### Kill what doesn’t change behavior
### Using Feedback and Killing Sections

At draft 12 [of *Leads*], I was like, "I'm done. This book's awesome." Then I socialized it with like 10 readers, and the feedback that I got, I was like, **"I have to rewrite the book."**

I use Stephen King's kind of method here: if many people have different comments about one section, their specific comments usually don't matter; it's more that there's something wrong with the section. In the third book that's coming out, every reader had something to say about this one chapter. I just cut the chapter entirely and then just edited the book to not reference the material in that chapter. As soon as I cut what was actually three chapters, one section, it was like reading downhill. Little things, if I agree with them, I'll quick fix. If I don't agree, I'll just ignore. But if many people comment on one section, I'll strongly consider deleting it entirely.

- generic praise words (world-class, cutting-edge, game-changing)
- scene-setting without a decision/action

- abstract nouns without examples

## 6) Newsletter / short-form

## Part 7: Newsletter Writing and Simplicity

**Interviewer:** Your newsletter has easily more than 100,000 subscribers, but it's the engagement that is worth talking about. How do you actually sit down to write the dang thing?

**Harry Dry:** Every newsletter genuinely is tough. Ann Handley has this great line: **"It's less about the news and it's more about the letter."** So when I start to write it, I think right, it's a letter.

How I do this is **time and place**. JK Rowling started *The Chamber of Secrets* with something like, "Not for the first time, an argument had broken out over breakfast at number four Privet Drive." So from that, you know it's breakfast, they're arguing, time, place, what's going on. That's how I start. Last one I did was: "Live from London. Weather update: trees shudder in majestic hail." I just looked out the window.

The actual meat of a newsletter, there are three things I do:
1.  **Density:** You get five or six examples in a newsletter. It's dense.
2.  **Conflict:** There's conflict in pretty much every single example (before/after, problem/solution). We as human beings remember stuff relatively. I want to give you pickle juice and orange juice. The pickle juice makes the orange juice taste a little bit sweeter. If I'm writing about Loom's positioning, I don't start with Loom's positioning is good. I start with: here's how every single other screen recorder positions their product. Here's how Loom does it. (They moved from "screen recorder bucket" to creating the "async video messaging" category).
3.  **Brevity:** Since March 2021, I haven't written a paragraph longer than two lines.

#### Why Short Paragraphs Work

* Short paragraphs are like **monkey bars**: they're easy to swing between. People read newsletters on the train; they're not that focused. You got to help them swing.
* If I write a paragraph which is three lines long, there's a good chance I'm not explaining myself as well as I could be. I'll look at it like, do we need that word? Do we need that sentence? No. Two lines.
* I like giving lines room to breathe.

#### Writing Simply Is Rewriting Simply

**Interviewer:** Out of everybody I read, I bet that your sentences are the shortest sentences. How do you write simply?

**Harry Dry:** Writing simply, what we're really talking about here is rewriting. You can't write simply, you can only rewrite simply.

My favorite rule for this is **Kaplan's Law of Words**: Any words that aren't working for you are working against you. I love Kaplan's Law of words so much I invented Harry's Law of Words, which is that you aren't taking Kaplan's Law of words seriously enough.

The strength of an idea is inversely proportional to its scope. This is why the word "and" on a landing page is normally never a good thing. "We sell jeans and t-shirts and socks." I buy jeans from Hiut Denim. Their tagline? **"We make jeans."** That's it. Do one thing well.

A good paragraph is like a burrito. I should be able to throw it to you and you should be able to catch it, and it shouldn't come apart in the air. A test for this: you got a paragraph, you pull one sentence out. A good paragraph should be broken. If you pull a sentence out and it still works, the sentence probably shouldn't have been in there in the first place.

Repurposing framework:
### **The Repurposing Framework**

A cornerstone of Alex Hormozi’s content empire is his highly efficient content repurposing strategy. This systematic approach enabled him to dramatically increase his content output from approximately 7 pieces per week to an astounding 80 pieces per week, all while personally dedicating only two days a month to content creation.11

His precise framework for content repurposing involves four distinct steps:

1. **Validate Content Concept:** Ideas are initially tested on platforms like Twitter to gauge audience resonance and minimize risk before significant investment in production.11
2. **Create Video Content:** Once validated, the concept is developed into long-form video, serving as the foundational “raw material” from which all other content formats are derived.11
3. **Team Edits and Contextualizes:** A specialized team then takes the long-form video and meticulously edits and adapts it for specific platforms and their unique audience behaviors.11
4. **Distribute and Monitor:** The repurposed content is then systematically distributed across various channels, with performance closely monitored to inform future iterations.11

This repurposing framework functions as a highly optimized “content factory” model. By validating ideas on a low-cost platform like Twitter 11 before committing to resource-intensive long-form video production, he effectively de-risks the entire content creation process. The long-form video then becomes the central “raw material” that can be efficiently processed by a specialized team into a multitude of platform-specific assets. This systematic approach allows for an exponential increase in content volume 11 without a proportional increase in his personal time, effectively resolving the common entrepreneurial challenge of needing to create extensive content while facing severe time constraints.11 This represents a masterclass in achieving operational efficiency within content marketing.

## 8) AI usage (guardrails)
## Part 8: AI, Taste, and Experience

**Interviewer:** How does AI show up in all of this for you?

**Harry Dry:** I have a great quote: "Switching from pens to typewriters didn't make the work better. Switching from typewriters to laptops didn't make the work better. Switching from film to digital didn't make the work better." **AI is a tool. It's only as good as your taste.**

I saw somebody on Twitter the other day using Photoshop's generative fill. They took the Beatles' *Abbey Road* cover, zoomed out, and generative fill added a big balloon in the air and loads more trees, completely uncropped. And they write, "AI revolutionizing the photography process." I'm thinking, God help you.

Ogilvy's Rolls-Royce ad: "At 60 MPH the loudest noise coming from the Rolls-Royce is the sound of an electric clock." He pulls that out of the motor magazine. Why does he pull that out? He's got taste. He puts it as the headline. That's conviction.

AI doesn't have taste, conviction, or experience.
* Bukowski worked as a postman for 20 years and wrote *Post Office*.
* Jack Kerouac lived on the road for seven years and wrote *On the Road* in three weeks.
* Michael Lewis spent forever working on Wall Street and then wrote *The Big Short*.

If you ask me to write about New York City, I've never been. So I would just say "yellow taxis," "city that never sleeps." I'm gumming together long strips of words already set in order by somebody else. Unfortunately, that's quite literally what AI does. It's predictive.

What's writing? You see something, you believe something, you write about it. An AI can't see anything and it doesn't believe anything. You ask AI what do you believe, it says, "I am a robot. I don't believe anything." So how are you going to tell me something surprising?

- Pass Dry gate? (visualize/falsify/only-you)  
- Any claim that needs proof?  
- Any paragraph that doesn’t change behavior?
